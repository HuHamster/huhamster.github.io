Mercury Tilt Sensor Project

Learn how to use a mercury tilt sensor with Arduino to detect changes in inclination.

Description

Discover the world of simple yet effective motion sensors with our "Mercury Tilt Sensor Project for Arduino." This compact component is an ideal starting point for anyone looking to integrate tilt detection into their electronic creations.

A mercury tilt sensor is essentially a miniature electrical switch. Inside, it contains a small amount of liquid mercury and two contacts. Due to mercury's high electrical conductivity, when the sensor's angle changes, the mercury shifts, either connecting or disconnecting the electrical circuit. This makes the sensor a straightforward yet reliable switch that has no polarity, greatly simplifying its connection.

This project demonstrates how easily you can integrate a tilt sensor with Arduino to control an LED or other devices, responding to movement and orientation. It's perfect for beginners and anyone seeking simple solutions for "tilted/not tilted" state detection.

Video Tutorial

Required Components

  • Arduino Board (e.g., Uno, Nano)
  • Mercury Tilt Sensor
  • LED (Light Emitting Diode)
  • 220-ohm Resistor (for LED current limiting)
  • 10 kilo-ohm Resistor (for pull-up configuration with the sensor)
  • Breadboard (optional, for easier prototyping)
  • Jumper Wires

Wiring Diagram

To connect the mercury tilt sensor, simply connect one leg of the sensor to the GND pin of your Arduino board. The other leg of the sensor should be connected to an Arduino digital input pin, for example, D5.

Additionally, you need to add a 10 kilo-ohm pull-up resistor. Connect one end of this resistor to the same digital pin (e.g., D5) where you connected the sensor's second leg, and connect the other end of the resistor to the 5V pin on the Arduino board. This ensures a stable HIGH reading when the sensor's contacts are open.

For the LED, connect its long leg (anode) through a 220-ohm current-limiting resistor to any Arduino digital output pin, such as D6. The short leg (cathode) of the LED should be connected directly to the GND pin on the Arduino.
